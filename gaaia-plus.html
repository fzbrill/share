GAAIA+

A redesign of the Great American AI Act (discussion draft) for an accelerating capability curve

GAAIA+ keeps the basic architecture of the Great American AI Act discussion draft: a federal standards body (CAISI), a market of licensed independent auditors, mandatory transparency and incident reporting, and whistleblower protections. It changes two things. First, it gives the safety floor real teeth and reshapes it so it can keep pace with fast-moving capabilities instead of falling a generation behind. Second, it strikes the draft's preemption of state AI law. The rest of this page describes those changes.

What stays

CAISI remains the federal body that sets standards and licenses auditors. Independent auditors still examine frontier developers and report to CAISI. Mandatory transparency, incident reporting, and whistleblower protections carry over, as does enforcement through licensing, revocation, and penalties.

The core change: a real floor that updates itself

In the discussion draft, large developers write their own risk frameworks and the auditors check whether a firm complied with the rules it wrote for itself. GAAIA+ replaces that self-graded standard with a government-set minimum floor that every frontier-capable firm must clear, and it makes participation mandatory for any firm capable of building a frontier model. To keep that floor from going stale as capabilities jump, the floor is redesigned as follows.

The redesigned framework

The floor, now a staircase. The minimum standard is no longer a single bar but a published ladder of capability levels, each carrying escalating requirements.

  • Measurement. A defined battery of capability evaluations with published scoring, run or overseen by the auditors, plus mandatory independent red-teaming. This both certifies a model and locates it on the ladder.
  • Requirements by rung. Lower rungs: weight security, incident reporting. Middle rungs (for example, meaningful cyber-offense capability or long-horizon autonomy): independent red-team sign-off, higher weight security, deployment limits. Top rungs (for example, bioweapon uplift, or a model that attempts to evade oversight in testing): no external deployment until specified safeguards are demonstrated, and a pause until then.

Automatic triggering. Crossing a rung is pre-committed. When eval results show a model crosses a threshold, that rung's requirements bind automatically, without a fresh rulemaking, so the floor updates at the speed of testing rather than the speed of legislation.

Setting and revising the ladder. CAISI writes the ladder in advance and revises the ladder itself on a regular schedule through an open, public process conducted in coordination with the frontier labs. The labs inform the ladder; they do not control it.

Stale-capability pause. A capability no existing rung anticipated does not inherit a pass from prior tests. It defaults to a pause until CAISI evaluates it and places it on the ladder, with the burden of proof on the firm holding the model.

Emergency interim floor. On evidence of a serious risk the ladder does not cover, surfaced through mandatory incident reports, auditor evaluations, or whistleblowers, CAISI may impose a binding interim requirement effective immediately. Each interim requirement carries a hard sunset and expires unless ratified through the full public process.

Control of the floor. The ladder and any change to it are set by a standing technical panel within CAISI that includes independent outside experts, published in full, and open to challenge in court. Audits stay distributed across several independent bodies. No single appointee can bend the floor.

Guaranteed auditor access. The auditors' access to frontier compute and model internals is guaranteed by law, so no lab can narrow it under competitive pressure.

What GAAIA+ strikes: the state-law preemption

The discussion draft's Section 121 preempts state and local laws that specifically target AI model development, sunsetting three years after enactment. GAAIA+ strikes it.

Removing the preemption keeps the federal floor a floor rather than a ceiling. States retain the ability to regulate frontier model development, and common-law and tort remedies stay intact, so a federal standard cannot be used to shut down stronger action elsewhere or to strip people of their existing right to sue.

GAAIA+ is a personal redesign proposal built on the Great American AI Act discussion draft. It is not affiliated with the bill's sponsors.
