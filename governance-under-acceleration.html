Governance Under Acceleration

The approach, and why

The approach I keep coming back to is the one I argued for in the last exercise: don't let a single government office do all the safety work, and don't let the companies grade their own homework either. The government sets a public safety floor and keeps it current; licensed independent auditors check that every frontier-capable firm clears it; and when the floor changes, it changes out in the open, in coordination with the labs, who inform the bar without setting it alone. The full framework is spelled out in the block below.

I picked it because it tries to have things both ways, in the good sense: real teeth and public accountability from the floor, but speed and specialization from independent auditors who can keep pace, even using AI to help them. On a normal timeline, that balance holds. The question here is whether it survives a timeline that doesn't.

The framework, as originally proposed

Scope. Participation is mandatory for any firm capable of developing a frontier model.

The floor. A government-set minimum safety standard in two layers. A measurement layer: a defined battery of capability evaluations with published scoring, run or overseen by the auditors, together with mandatory adversarial red-teaming by qualified independent teams following a set methodology. And a requirements layer: the safeguards, model-weight security level, and deployment conditions a firm must have in place. The evaluations establish what a model can do; the requirements establish what the firm must do about it. Every covered firm must clear the standard before developing or deploying a frontier model.

Setting the floor. CAISI sets and revises the floor through an open, public process, conducted in coordination with the frontier labs. The labs inform the standard; they do not control it.

Verification. Licensed independent auditors, granted access to frontier compute and model internals, test each firm against the floor. Clearing it is a precondition for deployment; failing it means no deployment.

Enforcement. CAISI licenses the auditors and can revoke a license for negligence; audits and violations carry public reporting and penalties.

What breaks

So what breaks first? The floor-setting process. Everything in the design hangs off that public floor; the auditors only check whether a firm clears it. So if the floor goes stale, the audits become theater no matter how sharp the auditors are. And the floor is the slowest-moving part of the whole system.

Here's the mechanism. Setting or raising the floor in the open, with public comment and coordination with the labs, takes months even when everyone hurries. In an intelligence explosion, capabilities don't wait months. A jump that used to take a year lands in weeks. So inside a single revision cycle, models leap two or three effective generations past the bar. The auditors keep certifying compliance, and those certificates are real, but they're grading models against a floor written for systems that are already ancient. Nothing turns red. Compliance stays green while the real risk climbs underneath it. The system fails quietly, which is the worst way, because everyone can wave a clean audit.

Then competition makes it worse, and it goes straight at the coordination step I built in for accuracy. Coordinating with the labs was supposed to keep the bar smart. Under a race it flips into the channel labs use to argue the bar down or slow-walk its revision. They have the leverage, because the government needs their cooperation and is scared of losing the lead, and they have the perfect line: raise the floor on us and you hand the race to China. The valve built for accuracy becomes the valve for capture.

The fix

The fix has to beat both failure modes, latency and capture, and it does it mostly by changing the form of the floor. The full redesign is in the second block below; here's the logic.

Turn the single bar into a staircase. Instead of one standard everyone is judged against, publish a ladder of capability levels, each with heavier requirements, the way Anthropic's ASL levels work. A model's eval results decide which rung it stands on, and at that rung it's still pass-or-pause. The move that actually beats latency isn't the shape, it's that crossing a rung is pre-committed: the eval result flips the switch automatically, so the floor climbs at the speed of testing instead of the speed of rulemaking. The slow public process still runs, but now its job is to write and periodically revise the ladder, not to chase each new model. Like a building code that requires a second fire stair above six stories, nobody relitigates it each time; crossing the line triggers the rule.

Two more pieces close the gaps the staircase leaves. First, the genuinely new: a capability no rung anticipated doesn't earn a pass just because it slipped past the old tests. It defaults to a pause until it's evaluated and placed on the ladder, and CAISI can drop a binding interim requirement immediately when something alarming shows up, with a hard sunset so emergency speed can't harden into permanent power. Second, capture: because the floor now moves fast and automatically, whoever defines the staircase holds real power, so control is split. The ladder is set by a standing technical panel with independent experts, published, and challengeable in court, and the audits stay spread across several independent bodies, so no single appointee can bend it. Pre-committing the ladder also strips the labs of their old leverage, since they can't argue the bar down model by model when the whole escalation was agreed to in advance.

The framework, redesigned to survive acceleration

Scope. Unchanged: mandatory for any firm capable of developing a frontier model.

The floor, now a staircase. The minimum standard is no longer a single bar but a published ladder of capability levels, each carrying escalating requirements.

  • Measurement. The same two-layer measurement as before (standardized capability evaluations with published scoring, run or overseen by auditors, plus mandatory independent red-teaming) now also locates each model on the ladder.
  • Requirements by rung. Lower rungs: weight security, incident reporting. Middle rungs (e.g., meaningful cyber-offense capability or long-horizon autonomy): independent red-team sign-off, higher weight-security, deployment limits. Top rungs (e.g., bioweapon uplift, or a model that attempts to evade oversight in testing): no external deployment until specified safeguards are demonstrated, and a pause until then.

Automatic triggering. Crossing a rung is pre-committed. When eval results show a model crosses a threshold, that rung's requirements bind automatically, without a fresh rulemaking. The floor updates at the speed of testing.

Setting and revising the ladder. The slow, open, public process no longer revises the bar model-by-model. It writes the ladder in advance and revises the ladder itself on a regular schedule, still in coordination with the labs, who inform but do not control it.

Stale-capability pause. A capability no existing rung anticipated does not inherit a pass from prior tests. It defaults to a pause until CAISI evaluates it and places it on the ladder. The burden of proof sits with the firm holding the model.

Emergency interim floor. On evidence of a serious risk the ladder does not cover, surfaced through mandatory incident reports, auditor evals, or whistleblowers, CAISI may impose a binding interim requirement effective immediately. Each interim requirement carries a hard sunset and expires unless ratified through the full public process.

Control of the floor. The ladder and any change to it are set by a standing technical panel within CAISI that includes independent outside experts, published in full, and subject to challenge in court. Audits remain distributed across several independent bodies. No single appointee can bend the floor.

Verification and access. Unchanged, except that auditors' access to frontier compute and model internals is guaranteed by law, so it cannot be narrowed under competitive pressure.

Enforcement. Unchanged: CAISI licenses the auditors and can revoke a license for negligence; audits and violations carry public reporting and penalties.

Ngo's lens: did the fix balance power, or just move it?

Ngo splits goal-seeking systems into two kinds. A centralized one has a single decision-maker at the top; it's coherent and decisive, but brittle, because if the center is wrong or captured, the whole thing goes over the cliff together. A distributed one is many semi-autonomous parts with no single boss; it's robust, meaning it holds up in situations nobody planned for, but it can fail to coordinate when a shared threat needs one hand on the brake. The trade is coherence against robustness, and the thing worth wanting is both at once, what Ngo calls coalitional. My redesign is itself a goal-seeking system, so I can hold it up against that axis, and it pulls in both directions on purpose. The real question is whether I landed in the middle or just relocated the danger.

The centralizing pieces are the ones that beat latency. One public floor every firm must clear. An automatic ratchet that can tighten requirements across the whole industry at once. An emergency interim floor that takes effect immediately. And a standing technical panel that, through those levers, can effectively halt frontier development. That is a small group with a very powerful brake, and it's decisive by design, because a purely distributed system can't stop a fast-moving catastrophe in time.

The distributing pieces are the ones that beat capture. No single appointee can move the floor; the panel is multi-member and includes outside experts. The audits are spread across several independent bodies. Every change is published and can be challenged in court. The whole authority sunsets and has to be re-justified. None of that is decorative; each piece exists so that capturing any one node isn't enough to bend the system.

Now his three questions, straight.

Does the fix concentrate power in ways that create new risks? Yes, and I won't pretend otherwise. The pause authority and the interim floor are real power, and in a world where being first matters enormously, the ability to halt a lab is also the ability to pick winners, or to be leaned on by whoever wants a rival slowed. Making the floor fast and automatic didn't remove that power, it sharpened it. Pre-commitment helps, because a rung agreed to in advance is harder to weaponize against one firm than a decision made in the moment, but it doesn't erase the risk.

Who oversees the overseers? Not any single thing, deliberately, because one overseer is just the chokepoint moved up a level. The panel is watched by the courts, since its actions are reviewable; by the public, since its rules and the evidence behind them are published; and by the legislature, since the sunset forces periodic reauthorization. The bet is that an abuse would have to survive all three at once, in the open. That's not a guarantee, it's friction, the same candid answer as in the first exercise. If one faction controlled the courts, the agency, and the legislature at the same time, this design wouldn't save us, and neither would any other.

If power is distributed this broadly, can it still coordinate against catastrophic risk? This is the uncomfortable one, because the checks and the decisive brake are in tension by construction. My answer is that the coordination lives in the pre-committed parts, not in a committee deciding in real time. The ladder and the automatic triggers are the coordination mechanism: they let the whole system act in unison the instant an eval crosses a line, without waiting for anyone to convene. The emergency interim floor is the release valve for the case the ladder didn't foresee. So the decisive action is pre-loaded in calm moments, and the distributed checks mostly operate after the fact, reviewing and ratifying rather than gating the emergency stop. That's the specific way I try for Ngo's both-at-once: coordinate through commitments made ahead of time, so robustness in the structure doesn't cost decisiveness in the crisis.

Where does it still lean wrong? Toward the center, under pressure. The emergency power is the weakest point, because it's the one lever that acts before the checks do, and a bad actor would reach for exactly that. The automatic sunset bounds how long it can last, but not what it can do while it's live. That residual is the price of being able to move fast, and it belongs in the brief as a cost, not something I can design away.

One-page brief

What GAAIA+ Needs to Become to Survive the Intelligence Explosion, and What That Costs

GAAIA+ is my fix-up of the Great American AI Act: mandatory participation for any frontier-capable firm, a public safety floor, and licensed independent auditors who check that every firm clears it. On a normal timeline it works. Under an intelligence explosion its slowest part, the floor-setting process, breaks first. Capabilities outrun the months-long public revision cycle, so audits keep certifying models against a bar written for systems two or three generations old, and the coordination-with-labs step turns into a channel for capture. To survive, GAAIA+ has to change in a few specific ways, and each change carries a real cost.

What it needs to become

  • A staircase, not a bar. Replace the single floor with a pre-published ladder of capability levels, each carrying heavier requirements, triggered automatically off eval results the way ASL levels work. The floor then moves at the speed of testing, not the speed of rulemaking.
  • A default pause for the unanticipated. A capability no rung foresaw does not inherit a pass; it pauses until it is evaluated and placed, with the burden on the firm holding the model.
  • An emergency interim floor with a hard sunset. On evidence of a serious risk the ladder does not cover, CAISI can impose an immediate binding requirement; it expires unless ratified through the full public process.
  • Split control. The ladder is set by a standing technical panel with independent outside experts, published in full and open to challenge in court; audits stay spread across several independent bodies; the whole authority sunsets. No single appointee can bend the floor.
  • Guaranteed auditor access. The auditors' access to frontier compute and model internals is locked in by law, so no lab can narrow it when competitive pressure rises.

What that costs

  • A slow, heavy setup. Writing the first full ladder, its thresholds, eval batteries, and red-team protocols is far more work than a single bar, and being slow at the start is dangerous in a fast takeoff. The likely accelerant is AI itself, which means the same force that threatens the framework is what would build it in time.
  • A competitive penalty. A default pause that fires on unverified capability will sometimes halt a US lab while a foreign rival keeps running. That is the direct, unavoidable cost of putting verification ahead of speed.
  • Concentrated power that can't be fully checked. The pause and interim-floor powers are decisive by design, which means they can also pick winners or be leaned on. Pre-commitment, publication, the courts, and the sunset add friction, not a guarantee. The emergency power is the sharpest residual: the sunset limits how long it lasts, not what it can do while it is live.
  • Dependence on eval science. The whole staircase is only as good as the evaluations under it, which can be gamed, go stale, and lean on scarce expert red-teaming. The floor inherits every weakness of the measurements it stands on.

Bottom line. GAAIA+ can be made to survive acceleration, but only by becoming faster, more automatic, and more powerful, and the price is a heavier setup, a competitive penalty, and a concentration of power held in check by friction rather than by any guarantee. The claim here is not that this is safe. It is that it fails more slowly, more visibly, and more correctably than the alternatives.
